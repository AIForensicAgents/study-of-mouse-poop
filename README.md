# study-of-mouse-poop
A concise, professional — and slightly cheeky — field guide and dataset framework for the study of mouse scat (droppings, feces) intended for educational and biological field-study purposes.

---

## Overview
This repository collects observations, photographic records, morphological notes, and best-practice guidance for identifying and documenting mouse droppings in the field. It is designed for:

- Ecologists, naturalists, and students conducting non-invasive surveys
- Educators building field exercises on small-mammal ecology
- Citizen scientists contributing occurrence and habitat data

Scope: photographic records, morphology descriptions, habitat/diet inference, ethical/safety guidance, and a growing gallery for annotation and training datasets. This is *not* a lab protocol repository for culturing or manipulating biological samples.

---

## Why it matters
- Mouse scat is an accessible, non-invasive indicator of presence and activity, helping map distributions without trapping.
- Scat morphology and content can provide insights into diet, habitat use, and seasonal behavior.
- Documenting scat can assist in monitoring invasive species, disease surveillance (at a high level), and ecosystem health.
- Well-curated photographic datasets enable research and teaching, and power image-classification tools for biodiversity monitoring.

## Morphology notes
A few professional pointers for describing mouse droppings consistently:

- Typical size and shape
  - House mice (Mus spp.) often produce small, pellet-like droppings: cylindrical to slightly tapered, roughly 3–8 mm long.
  - Note: size ranges overlap across small rodent species—use with caution.
- Color and texture
  - Fresh droppings: darker, glossy; older droppings: lighter, crumbly.
  - Diet and hydration influence appearance (e.g., seed-rich diet → harder pellets).
- Segmentation and ends
  - Pellets may be uniformly cylindrical or more tapered/pointed depending on species and individual condition.
- Imaging best practices
  - Include a scale (ruler or coin) and a color-neutral background.
  - Use diffuse natural light or a lightbox; avoid strong shadows.
  - Capture multiple angles: top-down and side profiles.
- Metadata
  - Record date, time, GPS coordinates, habitat notes, and observer name. Consistent metadata increases scientific value.

## Habitat / diet clues
Interpreting droppings in context:

- Seed fragments and husks → granivorous diet; common in fields, edge habitats.
- Fur, bone, or insect exoskeleton pieces → evidence of insectivory or scavenging (more common in some species or life stages).
- Location context
  - Near nests, runways, compost heaps, or stored food indicates commensal behavior.
  - In natural settings (rock piles, logs, grass tussocks), droppings often reflect wild mouse species.
- Caution: other small mammals (voles, shrews, juvenile rats) can produce superficially similar scat. Use combined lines of evidence (tracks, runways, habitat) before assigning species.

## Ethics / Safety
Safety-first, ethical fieldwork is mandatory.

- Personal protective equipment (PPE)
  - Wear disposable gloves and avoid direct contact with droppings.
  - Consider a mask (especially in enclosed spaces) to reduce inhalation risk from dust/aerosols.
- Minimizing disturbance
  - Do not disturb nests or handle animals to obtain scat.
  - Photograph in situ when possible; only collect samples if you have institutional approval and a clear, justified protocol.
- Biohazard awareness
  - Rodent droppings can carry pathogens (e.g., hantaviruses, leptospirosis). Do not attempt to culture, extract, or manipulate material without appropriate biosafety facilities and training.
  - Wash hands thoroughly after fieldwork and decontaminate equipment.
- Legal and ethical compliance
  - Obtain permits where required for sample collection or work in protected areas.
  - Respect private property and local regulations.
- Data stewardship
  - Remove or redact exact sensitive location data for endangered species when sharing publicly, if required by conservation guidelines.
